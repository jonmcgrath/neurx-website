The Problem

Aspiration has a ceiling.
We built what comes after it.

Venous thromboembolic disease kills 100,000 Americans every year. Existing catheter systems leave organized adherent clot behind — not because of poor engineering, but because of inherent limitations to possible suction force.

Residual clot is not an angiographic failure. It is an independent mortality predictor.

When adherent, organized thrombus is left behind after aspiration thrombectomy, the clinical consequences are severe and well-documented. Partially fibrinolyzed, cross-linked clot has fundamentally different physical properties than fresh thrombus. Vacuum aspiration cannot generate sufficient interfacial shear stress to dislodge it from the vessel endothelium — and this is a first-principles physical constraint, not an engineering gap.

Physicians accept incomplete removal because no device has been engineered specifically to solve this problem. Until now.

  • 4× Higher PE-related mortality in patients with residual thrombus burden after thrombectomy[1]
  • 2.7× Higher risk of adverse outcomes — including death, recurrent PE, or right heart failure — at 6 months in patients with residual pulmonary obstruction[1]
  • 10× Higher rate of VTE recurrence at one year in patients with significant residual thrombus[2]
Organized, adherent thrombus remaining on vessel wall after aspiration — the clinical problem the NeuRx Sweeper is engineered to solve.

The Aspiration Ceiling

Every thrombectomy aspiration system operates using vacuum that is limited to about −730 mmHg. This is sufficient for soft, fresh, mobile thrombus but is mechanically insufficient against organized, adherent clot that has undergone partial fibrinolysis and has become attached to the vessel wall. No pump engineering can overcome this constraint — it's an inherent limitation, not a design choice.
